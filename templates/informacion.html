What is AURA?

Aura is a biosensing system designed to measure brain activity using EEG (electroencephalographic) technology. It is composed of three main elements:

  • Software
  • A biosensor board
  • And an EEG cap with electrodes

Its goal is to make these technologies accessible for researchers, creatives, and scientists, by offering a more affordable alternative to traditional professional devices.

History of Aura

2018 – Aura 00:

Dr. Christian Peñaloza, with a PhD in cognitive neuroscience applied to robotics, found that EEG devices were too expensive (around $16,000 USD). For this reason, along with a group of experts, he developed Aura 00, managing to reduce the cost to $3,500 USD.

2019 – Aura 01:

In collaboration with the Mexican creative agency de la O design studio, the design of the EEG cap was improved. This version integrated more formal patterns and 3D printed electrodes.

Aura 02 (Recent):

Developed by former student Gerardo Soler along with the Mexican studio Soon Studio, this new version includes an EEG cap with screen printing to identify the position of the electrodes and a more resistant and thinner biosensor board casing, manufactured with Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) technology.

(Mirai Innovation Research Institute, 2022)

What is TBR?

The Theta‑Beta Ratio (TBR) is a metric obtained from an electroencephalogram (EEG) that compares the power of theta (4–7 Hz) and beta (13–30 Hz) waves in the brain:

  • Theta waves (4–7 Hz): associated with states of rest, drowsiness, or memory and learning processes.
  • Beta waves (13–30 Hz): reflect attention, concentration, and an active state of wakefulness.

The TBR is obtained by dividing the theta power by the beta power in specific scalp regions (e.g., frontal or at Cz).

What is it used for?

Cognitive Biomarker

  • An elevated TBR usually indicates lower executive control or reduced attention.
  • Its use has been proposed in disorders such as ADHD, anxiety, or cognitive impairment.

In ADHD

  • The NEBA system, approved by the FDA in 2013, uses TBR as a diagnostic aid in children aged 6 to 17 years.
  • Early research (Monastra et al. 1999) showed 86–98% accuracy, but subsequent meta-analyses found less consistent results.
  • Recent studies show low sensitivity; TBR does not adequately distinguish between children with and without ADHD.

Neurofeedback

  • Protocols that seek to reduce TBR through neurofeedback have shown improvements in self-control (e.g., anxiety, focus), although some studies in healthy individuals found null effects.

Fourier Transform (FFT)

This tool converts a signal from the time domain to the frequency domain.

What do you get?

  • On the X-axis: frequencies (Hz).
  • On the Y-axis: power or energy of each frequency (µV² or dB).

This allows you to see peaks in:

  • 6 Hz → indicates theta
  • 18 Hz → indicates beta

(The Normal Awake EEG, n.d.)

Why the Crown Array Works

Strategic Coverage of Key Regions:

The array covers three essential lobes:

  • Frontal (F3, F2, F4) → Executive processes, attention, decision-making.
  • Central (C3, C4) → Sensorimotor integration, important for rapid cognitive responses.
  • Parietal (P3, P2, P4) → Sensory processing, visual and spatial attention, and working memory.

Interhemispheric Symmetry:

It allows you to compare activity between the right and left hemispheres, which is key in studies of ADHD and other cognitive disorders, where an imbalance is often observed.

High Sensitivity to Theta and Beta Waves:

Frontal and parietal regions are especially sensitive to theta (4–8 Hz) and beta (13–30 Hz) waves, which are precisely those analyzed to assess attention levels or ADHD symptoms.

Scientific Basis:

"Frontal and parietal lobes are consistently associated with attention and working memory. Theta activity is dominant in frontal midline regions during focused attention, while beta rhythms are observed in parietal areas during active cognitive engagement (Klimesch, 1999)

Welch Method in EEG

The Welch method is used to obtain the energy of each signal at a frequency more precisely and with less noise. It obtains the Power Spectral Density (PSD) of a signal; in an electroencephalogram, it serves to obtain the PSD of theta, beta, etc.

The procedure is performed by dividing the signal obtained from the EEG into smaller parts (windows) to obtain more precise values. Additionally, it overlaps the windows with each other, for example, by 50% to avoid loss of information. After each of the windows, the Fourier Transform is applied to obtain the spectrum, and from there, they are averaged to obtain a more precise PSD.

(Low-Complexity Welch Power Spectral Density Computation, 2014)
